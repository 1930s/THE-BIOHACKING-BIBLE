How to isolate a gene of your interest from an organism


The organism is a species of plant.

Here are the scenarios:

(1) A heterologous clone of the gene of interest (cloned from a fungus species) has been obtained. The clone is in a plasmid and is sent to you as a stab culture of the transformed recombinant E. coli. The gene is supposed to be fairly conserved thru various species of organisms, but mispatches in the sequence between fungi and plants are to be expected.

(2) This is no genomic library made of the plant species, and since this plant has a very large genome, making a complete genomic library should be avoided.

(3) A definitive confirmation should be made that the corresponding gene from your plant of interest has indeed been isolated (i.e. a screen could be devised to insure that the gene has been cloned).
-----------------------------------------------
1. The genomic DNA of the plant is purified. The plasmid DNA of the transformed E coli is also purified. Then, the plasmid DNA and the plant DNA are digested with appropriate restriction enzymes (the same as the ones that the transformed E coli plasmid was initially cut for the insert of the specific gene, e.g. Eco RI and Bam HII) (Figure 1)

2. Run the digests on the agarose gel, perform a southern blotting and hybridization using the probe that has been known to target the gene from the fungus species. From Figure 2, the gene of interest in plant DNA could be found because it is quite similar to the gene from the fungus species.

3. Isolate the gene of interest, clone it, and amplify the clone.

4. Screen is performed by plating the culture of the new transformed recombinant E coli onto the agar plate containing ampicillin, and incubating the plate. If the target colonies could be seen, a definite confirmation could be made that the corresponding gene has indeed been isolated.
