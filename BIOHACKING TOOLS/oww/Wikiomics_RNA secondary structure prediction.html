==Single sequence structure prediction== A common problem for researchers working with RNA is to determine the three-dimensional structure of the molecule. However, in the case of RNA much of the final structure is determined by the [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_structure secondary structure] or intra-molecular base-pairing interactions of the molecule. This is shown by the high conservation of base-pair across diverse species. One of the first attempts to predict RNA secondary structure was made by Ruth Nussinov and co-workers who used dynamic programming method for maximising the number of base-pairs NUSS78. However, there are several issues with this approach, most importantly the solution is not unique. Nussinov et al published an adaptation of their approach to use a simple nearest-neighbour energy model in 1980 NUSS80. Michael Zuker and Patrick Stiegler in 1981 proposed using a slightly refined dynamic programming approach that models nearest neighbour energy interactions that directly incorporates stacking into the prediction ZUKE81. The energies that are minimized by the recursion are derived from empirical calorimetric experiments, the most up-to-date parameters were published in 1999 MATH99. There has been recent progress in estimating "energy" parameters directly from known structuresDO06. Another approach researchers are using is to sample structures from the Boltzmann ensemble MCCA90 DING03. One of the issues when predicting RNA secondary structure is that the standard recursions (eg. Nussinov/Zuker-Stiegler) exclude [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoknot pseudoknots]. Elena Rivas and Sean Eddy published a dynamic programming algorithm that could handle pseudoknots RIVA99. However, the time and memory requirements of the method are prohibitive. This has prompted several researches to implement versions of the algorithm that restrict the classes of pseudoknots, resulting in gains in performance. RNALOSS computes the number of k-locally optimal secondary structures for the input RNA, along with relative density of states and minimum free energy of a sample k-locally optimal secondary structure. ==Comparative structure prediction== Frequently researchers have more than one RNA sequence which they suspect are [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_%28biology%29 homologous] (or [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analogy_%28biology%29 analogous]) and therefore may share a common structure. There are three main approaches to this problem: 1. alignment folding. 2. simultaneous alignment and folding. 3. aligning secondary structures. For the alignment folding approach the [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutual_information mutual information content] is frequently used CHIU91. Generally the alignment is inferred using one of the standard [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_Sequence_Alignment multiple alignment] tools (eg. [ClustalW,] [MAFFT,] [MUSCLE]). Recently some SCFG based alignment folding algorihms have been developed, such as [http://www.daimi.au.dk/~compbio/rnafold/ PFold] KNUD03. Alternatively, a sum of energy and covariation terms can be used, such as [http://www.tbi.univie.ac.at/~ivo/RNA/ RNAalifold] HOFA02. ==== References ==== #CHIU91 pmid=1913217 #KNUD03 pmid=12824339 #HOFA02 pmid=12079347 ===Structures from alignments=== * [http://jaynes.colorado.edu/Bayes/ BayesFold] Finds, ranks, and draws the likeliest structures for a sequence alignment.
