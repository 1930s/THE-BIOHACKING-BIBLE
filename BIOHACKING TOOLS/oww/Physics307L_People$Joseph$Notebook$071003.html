This implies that there is a charge to mass ratio for the electron, which can be determined empirically. The accepted value for the charge to mass ratio of an electron is \frac{e}{m}=1.759\times10^{11} \frac{C}{kg}. ==Theory== Finding two expressions for the velocity of the electrons moving in the circular stream and setting them equal to one another will hopefully unveil a useful expression for e/m. ===First expression for velocity=== If the beam is moving in a circle, the velocity, v, can be discovered in terms of magnetic field strength (B), charge (e), radius of circle (r), and mass of electron (m). The force (radial) required to keep anything in circular motion is F_r=-\frac{m v^2}{r}. The force (radial) from the perpendicular magnetic field is F_r=-e v B \,. Solving for v in these two equations by first setting F_r=F_r gives v=\frac{reB}{m}. ===Second expression for velocity=== To create the electron stream, the electrons will be accelerated by a potential between two plates. The voltage between these plates, V, is the only significant means for the electron to speed up since the electric field practically goes to zero on the other side of the plate and since gravity is negligible with the speeds we are dealing with. Therefor, kinetic energy (K) equals the negative change in potential energy (\DeltaU) between the two plates, which equals charge (e) times the voltage between the plates (V): K=\frac{1}{2}m v^2=-\Delta U=Ve. Solving for v gives v=\sqrt{\frac{2Ve}{m}}. ===Why find these expressions?=== Setting both expression for velocity equal to each other and solving for e/m gives \frac{e}{m}=\frac{2V}{B^2 r^2}. This equation allows us to determine the \frac{e}{m} simply, considering only the parameters r, V, and B. ===An alteration to our result=== Instead of directly measuring the magnetic field (B, we derive what the B field should be in our experiment. If we have n circular loops of current of radius R that are wound parallel to one another, the magnetic field along the axis perpendicular to and in the center of the loops is given by B=\frac{\mu I R^2 n}{2\left(x^2+R^2\right)^{\frac{3}{2}}}, where \mu is magnetic permeability and x is the distance along the axis from the plane containing the loop. For any well-made Helmholtz coil, the distance between both coils is R, so, since the experiment is to be done half way between both coils, x=R/2. Also, since there are two coils, n=2N, where N is the number of loops in one of the two coils, the above equation becomes B=\left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^{\frac{3}{2}}\frac{\mu N I}{R}. Using N=130, R=0.15m, and \mu=\mu_0=4\pix10-7 Wb/(A*m), the Helmholtz coil being used abides (approximately) by the following equation at the point on the axis exactly between both coils: B=(7.8\times10^{-4} \frac{Wb}{A\cdot m^2})\cdot I. To derive this I have assumed that the electron beam will be on the axis, when, in fact, it will make a loop around the axis. However, this expression for B can still be used in the expression for e/m since the magnetic field weakens only slightly as one deviates from the axis as long as the deviation is small (not larger than R/2). And now, here's The Final Equation! \frac{e}{m}= (3.29\times10^6\frac{A\cdot m^2}{Wb}) \frac{V}{B^2 r^2}. ==Equipment== [[Image:Physics_307L_e_over_m1.JPG|thumb|All the equipment.|right]] *2 weak
