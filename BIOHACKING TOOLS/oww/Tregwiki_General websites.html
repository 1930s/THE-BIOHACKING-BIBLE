* Converters: ** [http://idconverter.bioinfo.cipf.es/ gene id converter] * Bibliography, News ** [http://barf.jcowboy.org/ BARF:] Get RSS-feeds for most biology/bioinfo journals here and put them into your... ** [http://reader.google.com Google Reader] to see everyday where new articles appeared in your subjects of interest, then look up bibtex with ** [http://www.jabref.org Jabref] Free Citation Manager for Latex ** [http://www.highwire.com highwire] Slower, but can show who's citing whom ** [http://scholar.google.com Google Scholar] Well, you know it, searches journals in fulltext ** [http://www.connotea.com Connotea] is Nature's bibliography and generic link exchange site. You can register, order your bookmarks and bibliography data, tag them and share lists with all other participants. ** [http://www.citeulike.org Citulike] is (as far as I know, TODO) almost the same thing. You can search both connotea and Citeulike for your keywords and possibly find similar articles in your domain. ** [http://www-tsujii.is.s.u-tokyo.ac.jp/medie/search.cgi MEDIE] looks pretty useful, a copy of pubmed with different operators, you specify e.g. certain verbs or sequences of words to search *Tools for Pubmed ** [http://www.hubmed.org hubmed] Faster, better than pubmed, can also export to bibtex and put the bibtex into... ** [http://www.glycosciences.de/tools/PubFinder/ Pubfinder] [http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=pubmed&dopt=Abstract&list_uids=15980583&query_hl=1&itool=pubmed_docsum (article)]: Give it a list of articles, it will extract the discriminative words and return similar articles ** [http://pubmatrix.grc.nia.nih.gov/ Pubmatrix] can compare a list of terms against a list of terms, searches all combinations and displays the results in a kind of "cross-matrix" view ** [http://www.chilibot.net/ Chilibot] can do multiple pubmed queries for you and displays nice graphics which word is occuring in which abstract. ** [http://www.gopubmed.org/ Gopubmed] launches pubmed-searches and sorts the results by Gene ontology. ** [http://www.ihop-net.org/UniPub/ iHOP] shows a gene-centric pubmed view. Type in any gene and iHOP will display all sentences that contain this gene together with other genes or MeSH terms. ** [http://clustermed.info/ Clustermed of Vivisimo] is a search engine like Google. Unlike google, it can display it's results clustered into groups which can make sense for pubmed. ** [http://www.ogic.ca/projects/xplormed/ XPloreMed] scan all abstract of a pubmed search and displays the most recurrent words in them. * Useless Pubmed Tools: ** [http://metnet.vrac.iastate.edu/browser/ Pubmed Friend] a Java interface for pubmed...well... ** [http://pmi.nlm.nih.gov/slide/index.php Pubmed Sliders] This is the same as pubmed, the only difference is that you can shift sliders to narrow down on your search results. Well... *Blogs: ** [http://www.nodalpoint.org Nodelpoint] Bioinformatics Blog, I guess the most famous one, in any case, this was the first one that I discovered ** [http://www.ghastlyfop.com/blog/index.html Flags and Lollypops] Bioinformatics Blog ** [http://blog.moseshohman.com/ Moses Hohman on Inforbiomatica] writes a lot about programming languages and bioinformatics and the best approach to develop software ** [http://fjossinet.u-strasbg.fr/ Propeller Twist] a lot about RNA, general stuff. ** [http://scienceblogs.com/goodmath/] Good math, bad math is a funny read, but nor really bioinfo-related ** [http://naturesnumbers.blogspot.com/ NaturesNumbers] is a much newer blog, less well-known ** [http://www.dalkescientific.com/writings/diary/ Andrew Dalke] an important developer for biopython, is also blogging from time to time * Programming, Statistics ** [http://www.r-project.org/ R] The statistics package. I merely use it for simple plotting. * Sequence Tools ** [http://www.mbio.ncsu.edu/BioEdit/page2.html Bioedit] The good old free sequence editor (Windows only). Outdated but still usable, especially for biologists. (Update Oct 05: Seems that the author decided to stop distributing it!!! You can still download it if you use google wisely...) ** [http://www.fruitfly.org/annot/apollo/ Apollo] and its siblings ACT/Artemis are not for everyday work, they are just great graphical interfaces to display annotations in any format (GFF, among others). ** [http://galaxy.cse.psu.edu/ Galaxy] looks very promising. Though far from straightforward, it allows every one without programming skills to run analyses on a genomic scale. I guess "extract of all coding regions and display a graph of their lenghts" is something that should be easy with galaxy...
