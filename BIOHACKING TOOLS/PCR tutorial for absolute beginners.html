PCR Tutorial for Absolute Beginners

Reassuring words

PCR is not alchemy, but when you start off it may seem a little 'magical'.  After a number of weeks or months, or sometimes to start with, unexpected results can be obtained, or it can fail altogether.  In this short PCR tutorial, I will attempt to give a basics-only knowledge of the reaction, and how to make it work.  I will talk about the theory of PCR, because you can read this in any textbook.  I would refer you to the seminal paper on PCR by Saiki R, K et al, "Enzymatic amplification of beta-globin genomic sequences and restriction site analysis for diagnosis of sickle cell anemia" Science, 1985 Dec 20, 230(4732):1350-4 which can be found here and the abstract here.  This tutorial will be practical and aimed directly and the lab bench.

There are seven components in a basic PCR reaction, which should be added in the following order:

Buffer, nucleotides, water, first primer, second primer, template and Taq.

The buffer contains a salt, usually KCl, as well as some magnesium chloride, which is a cofactor for Taq.  The nucleotides are deoxyribonucleotide triphosphates which are the building blocks of DNA (A,T,C,G).  Water should be of the highest grade, preferably from a water purifier such as those made by millipore called 'MilliQ' - you can always buy in PCR-grade water from Eppendorf (Cat 0032 006.302).  The primers bind the target and make the reaction specific to that target.  The template material is the DNA which the primers will bind which contains the target sequence.  The Taq is the enzyme which carries out the amplification reaction by extending the primers from their binding-sites on the target along the template.  We will now consider aspects of each component in a PCR reaction.

PROTOCOL FOR a 50 microlitre reaction

The following amounts and concentration should be adjusted after opimization, but provide a good starting point.

  1. Add 5 ul 10 x Taq buffer
  2. Add 1 ul 10mM dNTP mix
  3. Add 40.8 ul of water
  4. Add 1 ul of each primer (10 pmol / ul)
  5. Add 1 ul template - pipette well
  6. Add 0.2 ul of Taq (5 units / ul)
  7. Store on ice until transfer to pre-heated thermocycler

SOLUTIONS

Buffer

The buffer must be thawed completely before use, and then mixed.  The salts can form concentration gradients during freezing and thawing.  When the contents is liquid, vortex it hard for ten seconds, and have a good look at the tube to make sure there is no ice left.   I like to thaw all components in a polystyrene float in some sterile water in a beaker, it takes 5-10 minutes, rather than 20 minutes in air, or much longer on ice.  People who thaw reagents slowly on ice usually seem to be demotivated, and spend the hours it takes to thaw things drinking coffee.  If you pipette thawed liquid from the top of the tube's contents while most of it is still frozen, you are making a mistake - not only will it contain less magnesium chloride, by taking away water, it makes the contents more concentrated for both salt and magnesium chloride.  I always get "10 times concentrated" buffer containing 15mM MgCl2, although you can get the two separate for 'fancier' reactions, which is nearly never necessary.  Increasing the magnesium concentration reduces specificity, generally one might try a reaction at a final magnesium concentration of 1.5, 1.8, 2.1, 2.3, 2.5 mM.

Nucleotides

I always buy my nucleotides from Promega, which simplifies things.  Make or buy (Promega cat# C1141) a stock solution of 'dNTPs' in which each of the 4 nucleotides is at a concentration of 10mM.  This means I need to put 1 microlitre of dNTP (10 mM) in each 50ul reaction.  Again, thaw this well, and vortex well before use.  I usually buy a concentrated stock and dilute it myself (Promega cat# U1330) because its slightly cheaper.

Water

Although double-distilled water may be fine, always autoclave water.  Ultrapure water such as that produced by milliQ machines contains virtually no salts which could affect the PCR reaction adversely.  Autoclaving water does not get rid of contaminating DNA templates, so if the water is from a suspect source, autoclaving does not solve anything.  Serious problems with contamination of water by E.coli can happen in nearly all labs, if the bugs in question contain a plasmid in which you had previously cloned a target sequence, you will be in trouble.  Keep a set of glass bottles (eg. Schott /Duran) specifically for the purposes of ultrapure water storage.  Never dip a pipette into these bottles, always pour your 'working "solution" of ultrawater' into a microfuge tube.

Primers

I order my primers from Genosys because they're cheap, fast and good quality.  I order 5 micromole scale.  The primers are supplied lyophilized (dried) as an off-white pellet in the base of the tube.  On the side of the tube, it will tell you the amount of primer in the tube in nanomoles (eg. 78 nmol).  I always resuspend the primer in the same number of microlitres of TE (eg. 78 microlitres is used to resuspend 78nmoles) - 10 mM Tris-HCl, 1 mM Na2EDTA, pH 8.0.  This means your stock is at a concentration of 1 mM.  Vortex for about 1 minute, then spin down briefly in a microfuge.  I then go on to make a 'working solution' in water, at a final concentration of 10 mM, which is the same as 10 pmol / ul. 

Template concentration and quality

As a rule of thumb you want about 50,000 copies of whatever you are amplifying in your reaction to begin with.  If you are using a crude template, such as a colony, 1 microlitre of a suspension of a colony in 30 microlitres of water is sufficient. The final concentration of template should be less than 10ng per microlitre.  Otherwise the templates amounts listed below are a starting point.

Plasmid 50fg
Prokaryotic genomic DNA  0.1ng
Eukaryotic genomic DNA   50-100ng

Taq DNA polymerase

I use Sigma Taq, which is cheap, but I have used Gibco, Pharmacia, but I particularly liked Promega Taq with Buffer B which contains a detergent, probably Triton X100.  Get it from a reputable supplier, and use no more than 1 unit per 50 microlitre reaction, scaling down with reaction volume as with all the other components.  Add this last!

Thermocycles

Although it is not possible to predict what PCR cycling conditions will produce best results first time, the following is a rule of thumb which might be useful as a starting point. The annealing temperature can be calculated using Oligo 2002.

Initial cycle: 1 minute at 95°C
40 cycles of:
Denaturation: 1 minute at 95°C
Annealing: 1 minute at 50°C
Extension: 1 minute at 72°C
Final extension: 10 minutes at °C
The extension cycle should be extended by 1 minute per kilobase of DNA to be amplified, ie. a two minute extension is required for a 2kb target.

Primer design

Primer design is an art when done by human beings, and a far better done by machines.  Use one of the programs available on the portal to design your primers. You can partly insure against unexpected aberrant PCR products by searching the public gene databases for the primer nucleotide sequences using FASTA for example.  If there similar or identical sequences in the database, they may provide an alternative target during the reaction itself.  There are exquisite tools for the analysis of primers, which human beings can use, but take the output from machines generally takes primer dimer and primer self-dimers into account. Equipment Considerations

Tubes - size, thin walled.  PCR blocks usually take either upto forty 0.5mL tubes, ninety six 0.2mL tubes...there are blocks that take 1mL tubes and others that take 384 well plates.  For most applications 0.2mL tubes are ideal, since most reactions will be less than or equal to 50 microlitres in volume.  The downside is the smaller the tube, the lower the centrifugal force before it falls apart.  Techne sell relatively robust 0.5mL tubes which survive centrifugation at 10,000rpm in a bench-top 'fuge.  I prefer thin walled 0.2mL tubes because they seem to give good results on short thermocycling programs.

Use a heated lid cycler - don't bother with mineral oil, it can inhibit your reactions.  If you do use it, keep it in a cupboard - there is a lab 'myth'  that UV light causes the formation of free-radicals in the mineral oil, which will completely inhibit your reactions.  Besides, its messy, impossible to pipette reprocibly, absorbs too much heat, and a certain source of cross contamination.

Thermocycles - most applications require 30-35 cycles, depending on efficiency and template abundance.  Amplifying a cDNA might require 35 cycles, whereas checking for an insert in a plasmid might require 20 or fewer cycles.  I generally start with 94C for 45 seconds, annealing temperature for 1 minutes, 72C for 1 minute per 500bp of target PCR product.  The annealing temperature should be 5C below the melting temperature of the lowest primer.

Thermocycler   If you have money, use a DNA Engine (MJ Research) - I use a PTC-200 gradient block (about £5000), otherwise try to get a few on trial for 2 weeks to 'road test' them from a number of suppliers before you spend your grant money!  Remember - fast peltier blocks are best (3 degrees Celsius per second heating)! Gradient blocks allow you to use a range of annealing temperatures across the block, allowing fast determination of optima as well as allowing reactions requiring different annealing temperatures to be run at the same time.

Pipetting - Use a 10 microlitre pipette for small volumes, with 10 or 20 microlitre tips.  'Tip off' excess Taq - adding more will only reduce specificity.

Other Considerations

Everying is stored at -20 degrees Celsius or colder, or on ice when thawed for use.  Buy your reagents from reputable companies like Promega, Life Technologies (Gibco), and Genosys.
