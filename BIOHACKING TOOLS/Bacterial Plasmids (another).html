Bacterial Plasmids

 

Bacterial cells may contain a number of plasmids which have different and varied functions such as funtions for colicin resistance, symbiosis, nitrogen fixation, and tumour induction in plants. Among the first to be discovered -- soon after the discovery of the F plasmid -- were the resistance transfer factors, which have been extensively modified and adapted over the years since to become the modern cloning plasmids.

These resistant strains were odd in a number of way:

 

  • Multiple drug resistances appeared rather than single resistances
  • Chloramphenicol resistance was never found (by itself)

A clue to the mystery of these strains was found when a multiple-drug resistant strain of E. coli was identified. It turned out that the Shigella strains had obtained their multiple-drug resistant phenotype from E. coli.

We now know that resistance in these and similar cases is due to the inheritance of large conjugative plasmids (R plasmids) that carry genes for more than one antibiotic resistance. R plasmids code for their own replication, they have genes for their own conjugal transfer, and they have mobile genetic elements.

[K18.1]

(One might note, in passing, that -- even as early as the 1960's -- the use of antibiotics was considered commonplace and routine. Is it any wonder that we are now facing the consequences of 40 years of antibiotic abuse?)

 

Other Natural Plasmids

Some other naturally-occurring plasmids are:

[T4.1] T4-1

 

  • The colicin plasmids of E. coli. These are small plasmids which encode the genes to synthesize toxins -- colicins -- that will kill other E. coli cells. The host is not killed because the colicin plasmid also codes for an immunity function which protects the host cell.

  • The symbiotic plasmids of Rhizobium spp. are responsible for the formation of root nodules in susceptible plants.

  • The nitrogen-fixation operon genes of Klebsiella pneumoniae are carried on a large plasmid.

  • Agrobacterium tumefaciens will infect wounds in dicotyledonous plants and cause the growth of a mass of undifferentiated cells. This loss of control of cell growth and division is the result of the transfer of a section of DNA from a plasmid in Agrobacterium into the plant cells. The plasmid is known as a Ti (tumour inducing) plasmid. The DNA that is transferred may code for the synthesis of a number of plant growth hormones. When these genes are expressed in a plant cell they promote unregulated cell division.

Structure of Plasmids

Plasmids are typically circular dsDNA molecules which range in size from 2 Kb to 100 Kb. These plasmids will be supercoiled in the cell.

[4.1] 4-1

Plasmids can also exist as linear dsDNA molecules.

The spirochaete Borrelia burgdorfferi has at least 17 plasmids. 11 of these have recently been sequenced; 2 are circular and 9 are linear molecules. These linear molecules are covalently closed and have terminal sequences that function as telomeres.

[B4.1] B4-2

 

Plasmid Replication

Plasmids must be able to replicate independently of the host chromosome. They have their own origin of replication (ori). There are two general modes of replication: THETA Replication -- which is found in most of the common gram negative plamids such as ColE1, RK2, and F; and, ROLLING CIRCLE Replication -- which is found in some gram positive bacterial plasmids.

[4.2] 4-4 ***

Replication is central to the control of a number of important plasmid properties: HOST RANGE; COPY NUMBER; INCOMPATIBILITY; and MOBILITY.

Little is known about the mechanism of replication of linear covalently closed DNA such as the linear plasmids found in Borrleia burgdorfferi. Some recent evidence suggests that the model shown at right may be reasonable for the bacteriophage N15 prophage.

Replication requires a "protelomerase" which has been found to be required for maintenance of a linear plasmid because it acts as a resolvase of replicative intermediates. Repression of protelomerase results in the accumulation of circular head-to-head dimer molecules.

Nikolai V. Ravin, Taisia S. Strakhova, Victor V. Kuprianov (2001) The Protelomerase of the Phage-plasmid N15 is Responsible for its Maintenance in Linear Form. J. Mol. Biol. 312(5): 899-906.

 

Plasmid Host Range

Some plasmids are able to replicate in a limited number of bacterial species; they have a NARROW host range. Examples are ColE1, pBR322, pUC18 plasmids which are limited to E. coli and some closely related species.

Other plasmids are able to replicate in a wide range of bacterial species; they have a BROAD host range.

[K18.1]

The ability of a plasmid to replicate in many different species of bacteria means that many of the sequence signals for gene expression, control of gene expression, and for replication, must all work in many different species.

 

Control of Plasmid Copy Number

The number of copies of a plasmid can vary from 1 (the F plasmid) to over a hundred (pUC18). This number is a property of the plasmid itself and depends on the mechanism by which it regulates its own replication.

[T4.2] T4-2

We discussed four different mechanisms of control of copy number in the course. This description should complement that in your textbook.

 

Replication of ColE1 based plasmids

ColE1 is a small circular plasmid which codes for a 57 kDal protein toxin (colicin E1) which can kill other E. coli cells by depolarizing the bacterial membrane.

[4.3] 4-5

The ColE1 plasmid carries the following genes:

 gene  function
 cea  the colicin toxin
 imm  immunity protein
 kil  lysis protein
 inc  RNA I - incompatibility determinant
 RNA II  primer for replication
 rop  protein that regulates priming and copy number
 mob  proteins for mobilization during conjugation
 cer  maintains plasmid as monomers
 exc  exclusion protein

 

It also carries and origin of replication (oriV) and a region (bom) at which it can be mobilized for transfer to other bacteria.

Replication of ColE1 proceeds unidirectionally from oriV and depends on RNA II which is transcribed from a promoter 555 bp away from oriV. When this transcript reaches oriV, one of two possible fates is possible:

[K18.2]

 

  • The RNA-DNA hybrid is cleaved by RNaseH to generate a free 3'-OH end which serves as a primer for DNA polymerase I to initiate replication. DNA polymerase III takes over after a short distance.

  • The RNA-DNA hybrid is NOT cleaved by RNaseH and transcription continues nonproductively.

 

The choice between these fates depends on the formation of a specific secondary structure in the nascent RNA II transcript. If the structure forms correctly then RNaseH processing occurs correctly; if not, it does not.

Clearly, anything that interferes with the formation of the correct secondary structure will interfere with plasmid replication.

[4.4] 4-6

There is a specific RNA whose function is to do just that! RNA I is a 108 nt molecule which is transcribed -- in the opposite direction to RNA II -- from a promoter located between the RNA II promoter and the origin of replication:

 

 

Since RNA I is perfectly complementary to RNA II, it is able to form a stable RNA-RNA hybrid with it. If it does, then RNA II will not be able to function as a primer.

This is an example of antisense RNA regulation.

Replication of ColE1 also depends on the Rop protein.

Rop is a small protein (63 aas) which increases the rate of binding of RNA I to RNA II. If Rop is absent, plasmid replication will be more frequent.

The relative copy numbers of pBR322 and pUC18 illustrate the role of Rop perfectly. There are approximately 15 copies of pBR322 per cell; however, there are 50-100 copies of pUC18, which is derived from pBR322. The relevant difference between the two plasmids is the fact that pBR322 contains the rop gene but pUC18 does not.

Turnover of the RNAI molecule also plays a role in the control of replication of these plasmids.

RNAI is a rare example of a prokaryotic RNA that is polyadenylylated. It contains a short, 25-50 nt, long polyA tail. This tail acts as a starting site for exonucleolytic degradation of RNAI by polynucleotide phosphorylase, a degradation that is facilitated if RNAI has been cleaved by RNAseE.

[RNAI structure]

RNase E is an essential ribonuclease with a key role in the degradation and/or processing of RNA. In E. coli, RNase E is present as a membrane bound multicomponent complex called a "degradosome". The other major components are polynucleotide phosphorylase (PNPase), the RhlB RNA helicase, and enolase while the DnaK chaperonin protein, GroEL, and polynucleotide phosphate kinase (PPK) are minor components. The ratios of the major components may vary during different stages of cell growth though the RhlB helicase remains at close to a 1:1 ratio.

The C-terminal part of RNaseE appears to be responsible for binding these other components. However, these other components are not essential for RNaseE function since a truncated RNase E protein lacking the C-terminal half is sufficient for cell viability and for RNA degradation and processing in vivo in E. coli.

The degradosome has also been shown to contain RNA molecules such as RNAI and
fragmented rRNAs which are known to be substrates for RNase E . RNaseE can cleave RNAI at three or four internal sites. More surprising was an observation that degradosomes could also contain the10Sa/ssrA RNA/tmRNA.

The ssrA RNA is transcribed as a 457-nt precursor which is processed at both 5' and 3' ends to generate the mature tmRNA. Processing at the 5' end requires RNase P; processing at the 3' end requires RNase E, which cuts the precursor at three sites immediately adjacent to the CCA-3'end.

The role of polyadenylation in regulating copy number control of ColEI plasmids was discovered when a new mutation affecing plasmid copy number (pcnB) was discovered and shown to be the the same gene as that coding for a bacterial polyA polymerase. More recently, Sarkar, Cao and Jain (Mol Genet Genomics (2002) 268: 62-69 [PDF]) carrried out a study to identify additional polyA polymerases and, instead, discovered two unexpected regulatory molecules. (pcnB mutants display residual polyadenylation activity)

They started with a DpcnB strain of E. coli carrying a ColEI based plasmid which contained the lac operon. Expression of b-galactosidase is then an approximate measure of plasmid copy number. Colonies containing this plasmid were less blue than wild-type strains of E. coli carrying the same plasmid.

A genomic library of E. coli DNA was constructed in a p15 based plasmid. This plasmid has a replicon which is compatible with that of ColEI plasmids. The plasmid library was transformed into the DpcnB strain of E. coli carrying the lac operon plasmid.

A small number of colonies appeared to have a darker blue colour that the others. This indicated that the p15 plasmid was expressing some function which was interfering with the copy number control of the ColEI plasmid. The isolated and measure the b-galactosidase acticvity in 14 colonies and found it to be 1.5 to 3.6 times higher than in the 'control' colonies.

[table]

When total plasmid DNA was isolated from these 14 strains, they found that the copy number of the ColEI based plasmid was increased in 13 of the 14 cases. Restriction digestion of the p15 plasmids indicated that there were 6 different plasmids isolated. Sequence analysis revealed that the six plasmids fell into two groups - 4 plasmids belonged to one group; the other group consisted of two plasmids.

[plasmid_digest]

The smaller group both contained an insert with the rssB gene and the larger group was found to contain an insert with the eutE gene.

RssB is a response regulator protein whose function appears to be to present the sigma factor RpoS to the protease ClpXP for degradation. It is not clear why RssB should affect plasmid copy number.

EutE is similar to a Salmonella protein which functions in ethanolamine metabolism.

In order to confirm the role of these two proteins in regulating plasmid copy number, each was cloned into an expression vector, pET19. This vector permits T7 RNA polymerase driven expression of cloned reading frames.

When RssB was over-expressed in this way, plasmid copy number increased ~4 fold.

[expression1]

When EutE was over-expressed, there was no change in plasmid copy number. However, when the eutE gene was cloned in the reverse orientation (which was expected to have no effect), plasmid copy number increased.

[expression2]

Therefore, it was not eutE that affected plasmid copy number, but something encoded on the other strand and expressed in the opposite orientation. Analysis of this strand, revealed a 16 nt sequence that was complementary to RNAI in 15 of the 16 positions. It is possible that this noncoding RNA regulates plasmid copy number by sequestering RNAI and preventing it from interacting with RNAII. However, there were a number of unexplained observations so this remains just a hypothetical mechanism.

[model]

Replication of R1 plasmids

R1 is a large (100 kb) broad host-range plasmid which replicates unidirectionally from a single origin of replication (oriR). Plasmid replication -- and hence, copy number -- is also controlled by an antisense RNA mechanism, though the mechanism is different from that of ColE1.

R1 requires a special plasmid-encoded protein, RepA, to initiate replication. RepA binding is required to permit DnaA to bind to th origin which in turn allows DnaB to bind and start the assembly of a replisome. RepA is very unusual because it acts only in cis. It is thought that RepA must bind to oriR as it is being synthesized and folded. A DNA bending sequence is located between repA and oriR which may facilitate this direct interaction.

[K18.6]

Since RepA is essential for replication, control of RepA expression will regulate replication and plasmid copy number. This expression is controlled in two ways: by the copA antisense RNA; and, by the CopB protein.

The organization of these elements is shown below:

 

[4.5] 4-7 ***

Expression of RepA occurs primarily as a result transcription from the repA promoter. However when the R1 plasmid enters a cell for the first time, RepA can also be expressed due to transcription from the copB promoter.

Transcription from the repA promoter is negatively regulated by the CopB protein, which is a small -- 10 kD -- protein.

Expression from the repA promoter is regulated by the copA RNA. This small -- 91 nt -- antisense RNA is complementary to the repA transcript and it will hybridize with it. When it does so, it sequesters the Shine-Dalgarno ribosome binding site sequence so that the 16S rRNA in the ribosome is unable to be positioned correctly and translation of the mRNA does not take place.

This mechanism of antisense RNA control -- i.e. prevention of translation -- is found in a number of systems.

[4.8] 4-12 [4.9] 4-13

[partitioning]

 

Replication of pSC101

pSC101 is the best-studied example of a plasmid whose replication is controlled by iterated RepA binding sites - ITERONS.

RepA is required for replication of these plasmids. It binds to several iterons in the vicinity of the origin of replication:

[4.6] 4-8 [K18.5]

2 sites are inverted repeat sequences that are located near the repA promoter. RepA binding to these two sites will inhibit its own expression.

3 other sites are 18 bp tandem repeats. These sites may serve to sequester RepA so that it is not available for replication possibly by linking or "handcuffing" plasmids containing these sequences together so that they cannot replicate.

Some experiments that support this handcuffing model are described in your textbook.

[16.4] 4-10 [4.7] 4-9

 

 

Replication of colIb-P9 plasmids

Replication of ColIb-P9 plasmids is also regulated via an antisense RNA mechanism - but it works in a different way to that found in ColEI and R1 plasmids.

ColIb-P9 is a large (93 Kb) low copy-number plasmid which replicates autonomously and whose replication required the RepZ protein. RepZ is expressed from its own promoter. An antisense RNA is expressed from the inc promoter.

 

At first glance, it might appear that this is a classical antisense RNA control system. However, the Shine-Dalgarno sequence of the repZ mRNA is not sequestered by the inc RNA. Nevertheless, down mutations in the inc promoter cause a drop in the amount of inc mRNA, an increase in the amount of RepZ protein, but no change in the amount of repZ mRNA.

Thus, expression of RepZ is controlled at the level of translation even though it is not by means of blocking the Shine-Dalgarno ribosome binding site.

Translation termination of repY, a small gene immediately upstream of repZ is critical for this control.

  • If the stop codon for repY is removed (by changing it to a sense codon, for example), or

  • If an amber mutation is created within repY so that it terminates upstream of its normal site

then translation of repZ is abolished.

It turns out that the stop codon of repY is just after the start codon of repZ. A ribosome that stalls at this stop codon can easily jump to the start of repZ. So, as long as repY translation stops in the correct place, repZ translation can start properly.

 

Translation of repY also permits a second event to occur. The repZ mRNA can form a stem-loop structure. A 4 nt sequence within the loop of this structure is complementary to a sequence immediately upstream of the repZ Shine-Dalgarno sequence. These two sequences can anneal to one another forming a structure called a pseudoknot. Formation of the pseudoknot and translation of repY depends on one another because they prevents competing reactions from taking place. Plasmids which carry mutations in the 4 nt sequence that destabilize pseudoknot formation are replication-defective. These mutations can be complemented by compensating mutations in the 4 nt complementary sequence upstream of the repZ Shine-Dalgarno site.

 Figure adapted from Fig. 1 of Asana & Mizobuchi (1998) J. Biol. Chem. 273: 11815-11825.

 

The stem-loop formed by the repZ mRNA can also anneal with the complementary stem-loop formed by the inc RNA in a manner similar to that between RNAI and RNAII of ColEI plasmids. If this happens, then the Shine-Dalgarno sequence of repY is sequestered and it cannot be translated.

In addition, the repY portion of the repZ mRNA can form secondary structures that sequester the RepZ Shine-Dalgarno sequence. Translation of repY prevents these from forming.

 

 

Incompatibility Groups

In the above mechanisms for the control of plasmid copy number, trans-acting molecules were responsible for the control of plasmid replication. For example, in the case of ColE1 plasmids, RNA I acts in trans. In the case of R1 plasmids, the copA RNA and CopB both act in trans.

If a cell contained two different plasmids, each of which had the same mechanisms of control, then because control is exercised through trans-acting molecules, each plasmid would be able to control the replication of the other. The inevitable consequence of this is that one of the two plasmids would eventually be lost from the cell simply as a result of random partitioning of plasmids into daughter cells during cell division.

[4.10] 4-14

Thus the two plasmids would appear to be incompatible.

Plasmids which have different mechanisms of control would replicate independently of one another and each would be partitioned between daughter cells. Thus, both plasmids would be maintained.

This is a basis for classifying plasmids. We can organize them into INCOMPATIBILITY GROUPS. Two plasmids in the same incompatibilty group cannot coexist in the same cell. The following table lists some incompatibility groups and plasmids.

Plasmid Transfer

Some plasmids can transfer themselves between bacterial cells through bacterial conjugation. These plasmids encode all of the protein functions that are necessary and required to carry out mobilization and transfer from a host cell to a recipient cell.

Many plasmids are unable to do this. They do not code for the necessary proteins themselves. However, if they coexist in a cell with a self-mobilizable plasmid, then they may be able to be mobilized if they carry the correct sequences or sites that are recognized by the proteins that are synthesized from the other plasmid.

[5.4] 5-8 ***

The Col E1 plasmid is a good example of a mobilizable plasmid. It contains a bom (basis of mobility) region with a nic site, which is nicked by proteins coded in the mob (mobility) region. Upon bacterial mating, the nicked strand forms a complex with a mob protein which leads it through a mating channel into a recipient cell.

[4.3] 4-5

 

TRIPARENTAL MATING

Molecular biologists make use of plasmid mobilization in a technique called triparental mating.

[5.10] 5-9 ***

Because it is difficult to transform may species of bacteria, conjugation often offers the only practical method of introducing new DNA -- particularly DNA which has been engineered with some genetic purpose or test in mind.

However, mating strains are also not commonly used for cloning DNA. The presence of other plasmids makes it difficult to purify plasmid DNA routinely.

Triparental mating allows plasmids to be contructed and propagated in one strain (A), and then mated with a broad-host range plasmid containing strain (B). Once the broad-host range plasmid has conjugated into strain A, it can now mobilize the engineered plasmid into another strain (C).

 

 

Modern Cloning Plasmids

Modern cloning plasmids are derived from various combinations of naturally occurring plasmids or other genetic elements.

pBR322

pBR322 was the first popular modern cloning vector. It was constructed by Ray Rodriguez and Herbert Boyer from three basic parts:

 

  • the tetracycline resistance gene segment was derived from the plasmid pSC101 (constructed by Stanley Cohen) which was, in turn, derived from the broad host range conjugative plasmid, R6.

  • an ampicillin resistance gene segment was obtained from the transposon, Tn3.

  • the origin of replication segment was obtained from pMB9 (constructed by Mary Betlach), which was, in turn, derived from the colicin plasmid, ColEI.

 

pUC plasmids

The pUC series of plasmids were all constructed by Jeffrey Vieira and Joachim Messing and all are derived from pBR322. The tetracycline resistance gene region of pBR322 has been replaced by a gene segment coding for part of the b-galactosidase enzyme into which a series of unique restriction sites have been designed (the multiple cloning site, MCS).

In making this construction, part of the segment containing the origin of replication was deleted. As a consequence, replication of the pUC plasmids is not properly controlled so that we have a higher copy number of the plasmids in the cell.

 

 

Shuttle Vectors

Plasmids with narrow host ranges will only replicate in certain bacteria. In particular, most of the modern cloning vectors are derived from the Col E1 plasmid which has a narrow host range. Thus if you want to do molecular genetics in strains outside this range, you have to either (a) construct a new set of vectors suitable for general cloning in your chosen strain, or (b) modify one of the common vectors so that it will also replicate in your chosen organism.

The second option is the easier option since all it requires is the addition of an origin of replication which will function in your chosen organism. Such vectors, with two different origins of replication suitable for two different bacteria, are called shuttle vectors.

[4.18] 4-20
