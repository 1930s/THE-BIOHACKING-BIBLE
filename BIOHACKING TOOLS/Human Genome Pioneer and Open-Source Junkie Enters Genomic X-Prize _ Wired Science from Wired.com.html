Human Genome Pioneer and Open-Source Junkie Enters Genomic X-Prize

By Brandon Keim December 04, 2007 | 2:58:27 PMCategories: Genetics, Lab Life, People  

Harvard geneticist and Human Genome Project leader George Church has entered a team in the Archon X PRIZE for Genomics contest. He's an early favorite in the $10 million contest, and -- good news for the rest of us -- he's an open-source software fan.

Winners must fully sequence 100 human genomes in less than 10 days for less than $10,000 a pop. The targets are important landmarks on the path to inexpensive genome sequencing and the promises of affordable, widely-available personalized medicine.

Though companies like 23andMe and Navigenics have already entered the genomic health market, their $1000 scans focus on chunks of DNA known to vary from person to person. Charting every last nucleotide is expensive and time-consuming; last year, X PRIZE competitor and Roche subsidiary 454 Life Sciences was celebrated for sequencing James Watson's genome in four months at a cost of $1 million.

Church's entry is -- to use strained sports analogies -- like Joe Torre or Terry Francona entering a team in a baseball tournament. Church is a genetics superstar, and his so-called Personal Genome X-Team includes an aerospace engineer, a physicist, a genomics professor and sequencing wonk. Best of all, they're making the plans for their next-generation gene sequencer freely available on the web, encouraging cooperation and hopefully ensuring that cheap sequencing will belong to everyone.
